This is the first post of the blog. Before any technical content, it's worth recording the road that led here. Not out of nostalgia: because the story explains most of the choices that will show up in later posts.

## The dream that wasn't mine

Since I was a kid I said I'd go to medical school. It took me a while to realize that was much more my mom's dream than something coming from me. When the entrance exam came, I didn't get into medicine. I got into Telecommunications Engineering and moved forward without any idea of what that meant.

Until that point I had never touched programming. Not once.

## The first Programming I class

First semester: a lot of calculus, a lot of new things, and me kind of lost trying to find myself as a person in the world. Then came Programming I.

The basics. `if`, `for`, a calculator on the terminal. I know it sounds small. But I was floored. In that moment I felt found. Could've been just first-semester epiphany. It wasn't.

I started pulling all-nighters writing CPF validators in C, computing Bernoulli, dumping code into [Beecrowd](https://www.beecrowd.com.br/) until the pagination ran out. It was the first time in my life that I actually wanted to study.

## Intelbras: the job that bought me time

At the start of the second semester I joined the Intelbras call center as a tech-support intern. Six hours a day, decent pay for an intern still living with his parents. I loved it at first: good company, good team, money coming in, and hours left over to study.

It wasn't what I wanted to do with my life, but it bought me the scarcest thing when you're starting out: time. Time to read, time to fail, time to build.

## Fundamentals turning into tools

Second semester brought data structures, sorting algorithms, Big O, binary trees. I got hooked.

By the end of the semester I built my first real project: a library simulator in C with a hash table and a numeric terminal menu (create account, sign in, read a book). It was simple, it was ugly, it was mine. I had bigger ideas, but I didn't have the right tools yet.

Third semester brought OOP with Java. Patterns, best practices, encapsulation. I learned a lot, but something was missing. I wanted to see things **happen**: make a request, consume a public API, expose my own. I didn't even know the name for what I was looking for.

It was through research that I landed on Spring Boot. That's when it clicked. First REST APIs, then Docker, databases, MVC, the whole vocabulary that's daily for me now.

## The decision

By the start of fourth semester, things were clear: I wanted to be a software engineer. Architecture, systems, technical decisions.

In the same semester an exchange-program slot opened up: a computer-vision project focused on elderly physical health. I applied. (That project deserves its own post, IOU.)

Before I even left, I was already exhausted at the call center. A year and a half of tech support had been enough.

## The exchange: a turning point

Three months abroad, full-time, doing software development, computer vision, and studying AI with actual time to dig in. It was the first time code was my job, not my hobby.

It was a turning point. I came back more certain than ever about where I wanted to be.

## The scholarship that changed the course

As the exchange was wrapping up, I saw an email at IFSC: a research scholarship on AI applied to the Brazilian electricity sector. I applied just "to see what happens". It worked out.

I started working on AI agents, multi-agent systems, and a lot of data analysis for the electricity sector. Things that, two years earlier, I didn't even know existed.

## Today

Seven months into the scholarship I was hired as an intern by the company that funded the research, with the title AI Software Engineer Intern. That's where I am now.

Every day I'm more sure that this is what I want to do.
